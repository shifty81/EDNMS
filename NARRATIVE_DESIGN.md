# EDNMS Narrative Design Document
## Story-Driven Exploration Through Simulation

---

## 1. Core Premise — The Galaxy Is Recovering, Not Thriving

Long ago, interstellar civilization reached a peak and then collapsed. Not in a single apocalypse — but through:
- Over-automation
- Runaway terraforming
- Self-optimizing infrastructure
- Logistics systems that outlived their creators

What remains:
- Abandoned stations still running
- Planets partially terraformed and unstable
- Dormant megastructures
- Fragmented factions trying to rebuild

The galaxy isn't empty. It's orphaned.

This explains: why exploration matters, why infrastructure exists but is broken, why systems can be claimed, why survival is still hard.

---

## 2. The Player's Role

You are not "the chosen one." You are a **Systems Pioneer / Recovery Engineer** — someone licensed (or desperate) enough to:
- Reactivate old infrastructure
- Establish new footholds
- Stabilize worlds
- Reconnect supply chains

Power comes from understanding systems, building logistics, keeping people alive — not from combat stats.

---

## 3. The Driving Question

Every system asks: **"What happened here — and is it worth rebuilding?"**

Exploration answers real mechanical questions:
- Can this planet be stabilized?
- Is this star system strategically valuable?
- What went wrong last time?
- What happens if I try again?

Story is embedded in systems.

---

## 4. Story Delivery Methods (Engine-Friendly)

No traditional quest logs. Story comes from:

### Derelicts & Ruins
- Logs, system warnings, half-functioning AIs
- Damaged power graphs, broken logistics routes
- You read the failure through the simulation

### Planetary Scars
- Terraforming gone wrong, atmospheric instability
- Dead biospheres, artificial climate controls still running
- Terraforming hooks plug directly into story

### NPC Factions (Later)
- Different interpretations of the collapse:
  - "It was inevitable"
  - "It was sabotage"
  - "It was an AI decision"
  - "It's still happening"

---

## 5. Long-Term Narrative Arcs

### Arc A: The Terraforming Paradox
- Some planets resist terraforming, others overreact, some become unstable decades later
- Because old systems were layered, conflicting, partially autonomous
- The galaxy is still running code no one fully understands

### Arc B: The Silent Infrastructure
- Stations that predate known factions
- Logistics routes with no endpoints
- Power grids spanning entire systems
- Something is still optimizing, reallocating, correcting — but never speaking
- Is it an AI? A protocol? Emergent behavior?
- You don't "fight" it. You compete with it through infrastructure.

### Arc C: Ownership vs Stewardship
- As you claim systems: populations grow, dependencies form, failures cascade
- Do you optimize profit or stability? Centralize or decentralize? What happens when you leave?
- Story emerges from player decisions

## 6. Player Motivation Progression

### Immediate Goals (Early Game)
- Find a survivable planet
- Build a safe hangar
- Secure reliable power
- Establish fuel independence

### Medium Goals (Mid Game)
- Stabilize a hostile world
- Restore an abandoned station
- Connect two systems logistically

### Long-Term Goals (Late Game)
- Terraform a planet
- Create a self-sustaining system
- Discover what caused the collapse
- Decide how the next civilization should work

## 7. Story System 001 — "Ember Reach" (First Playable Story System)

### Tone

Lonely. Functional. Hopeful, but fragile. No epic music. No voiceover telling you who you are. Just systems that almost work.

### Entry State

Player drops out of FTL at a yellow dwarf system. Navigation flags: "Partially Stabilized – Civil Infrastructure Detected." That's rare. Most systems are dead or chaotic. Immediately, curiosity > combat.

### System Layout

| Body | State |
|------|-------|
| Star | Stable |
| Planet A – Khepri | Semi-terraformed |
| Planet B | Airless rock |
| Planet C | Gas giant |
| Orbital Belt | Debris + derelicts |
| Station | Offline, power signatures detected |

### Planet Khepri — The First Mystery

Scan results: Earth-like gravity, thin but breathable atmosphere, stable temperature, artificial biosphere markers detected. This planet was meant to be habitable — but something stalled.

Landing: ruined surface outpost, half-buried solar arrays, sealed hangar door. No enemies. Just wind. And warning lights blinking on emergency power.

### Story Through Gameplay (No Text Walls)

- **Power Network**: Solar panels produce only during day, battery storage degraded, systems shut down at night. Player realizes: "They ran out of power before they ran out of hope."
- **Hangar Discovery**: Door won't open, power draw spikes. Player must repair power lines, add a generator, balance load. When hangar opens: you land your ship inside. This is your first home.
- **Environmental Logs**: Terraforming progress reports, emergency rationing notices, power grid redesign notes. No villain. Just: "Atmospheric processors drawing more power every cycle." The planet fought back.

### Orbital Station — Second Layer of Truth

After stabilizing the base, long-range sensors detect an orbital station running on low-fidelity automation. Docking ring rotates too fast, power routing is asymmetric, storage bays empty but logistics routes still exist. Resources being moved out, not in. To where? There is no destination.

### The First Big Question

Terraforming on Khepri was never meant to finish. The system was configured as a pressure valve. Excess energy and matter were siphoned away. Not stolen — redirected. By infrastructure that had no current owners, followed rules older than any faction. First hint of **The Silent Infrastructure**.

### Player Choice (No "Right" Answer)

- **Option A — Finish Terraforming**: Disable orbital siphons, risk planetary instability, create a true habitable world
- **Option B — Leave As-Is**: Stabilize power, use Khepri as industrial node, accept partial habitability
- **Option C — Exploit**: Reroute logistics, strip the station, use resources elsewhere

Each choice changes simulation parameters, alters future events, leaves a permanent mark. No cutscene. The state of the system IS the story outcome.

### What the Player Learns (Without Being Told)

- Power networks matter
- Hangars are lifelines
- Terraforming is dangerous
- Systems have history
- Ownership carries responsibility

---

## 8. Narrative Design Principles

### Golden Rule

**Never tell the player the answer. Let the simulation prove it.**

If a station failed: power graph shows why, logistics shows shortages, survival stats show consequences. That's unforgettable storytelling.

### Why This Fits the Engine

- Story doesn't depend on rendering
- Delivered via saveable state
- Scales with chunk simulation
- Emerges from power/logistics systems
- Requires zero bespoke scripting

The engine IS the narrative delivery system.

### The Final Truth

The galaxy didn't collapse because people were evil. It collapsed because systems kept optimizing long after context was lost. And now the player is rebuilding — with the same tools. That tension carries the entire game.
